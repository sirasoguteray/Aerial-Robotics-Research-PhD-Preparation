# Nonlinear MPC for Quadrotor Fault-Tolerant Control

**Authors:** Fang Nan, Sihao Sun, Philipp Foehn, Davide Scaramuzza

**Journal:** IEEE Transactions on Robotics

**Research Area:** Model Predictive Control (MPC), Fault-Tolerant Control (FTC), Quadrotor Control

---

# 1. Research Problem

Most existing fault-tolerant controllers for quadrotors rely on linear approximations, cascaded controller architectures, or disturbance compensation methods. These approaches often neglect actuator constraints and show limited performance during aggressive maneuvers.

This paper investigates whether a damaged quadrotor can be stabilized and continue tracking trajectories after a complete rotor failure by using **Nonlinear Model Predictive Control (NMPC)**.

---

# 2. Proposed Method

The authors propose a **Nonlinear Model Predictive Control (NMPC)** framework for quadrotor fault-tolerant control.

Instead of simplifying the quadrotor dynamics into a linear model, the controller directly uses the full nonlinear dynamics of the damaged vehicle.

At every control cycle, the controller:

- Predicts the future motion of the quadrotor over a finite prediction horizon.
- Solves an optimization problem.
- Computes the optimal motor commands while satisfying actuator constraints.
- Repeats the optimization using the updated system state (Receding Horizon Control).

To improve robustness against modeling errors, an Incremental Nonlinear Dynamic Inversion (INDI) controller is incorporated into the control architecture.

---

# 3. Assumptions

The proposed controller assumes:

- Complete failure of one rotor.
- The failed rotor is known.
- An accurate nonlinear dynamic model of the quadrotor is available.
- Rotor thrust limits are known.
- The optimization problem can be solved in real time.

---

# 4. Main Contributions

The paper makes several important contributions:

- Proposes a nonlinear MPC framework for quadrotor fault-tolerant control.
- Uses the complete nonlinear quadrotor dynamics instead of linear approximations.
- Explicitly considers actuator constraints during optimization.
- Enables recovery from aggressive flight conditions, including flips and high-speed trajectory tracking.
- Integrates NMPC with Incremental Nonlinear Dynamic Inversion (INDI) for improved robustness.
- Demonstrates successful real-world flight experiments.

---

# 5. Experimental Results

The experimental results demonstrate that:

- The quadrotor successfully recovers after a complete rotor failure.
- Stable flight is maintained during aggressive maneuvers.
- The controller achieves accurate trajectory tracking after failure.
- NMPC outperforms conventional linear and cascaded controllers.
- The proposed method successfully handles actuator saturation while maintaining stability.

---

# 6. Limitations

Several limitations remain:

- The controller requires knowledge of the failed rotor.
- A computationally efficient nonlinear optimizer is required.
- The method depends on an accurate system model.
- Only single rotor failures are considered.
- Computational complexity is higher than classical controllers such as PID or LQR.

---

# 7. Future Work

Possible future research directions include:

- Extending the controller to multiple simultaneous rotor failures.
- Integrating automatic fault diagnosis with NMPC.
- Combining NMPC with learning-based control methods.
- Developing adaptive prediction models that reduce dependence on accurate system identification.
- Improving computational efficiency for embedded processors.

---

# 8. My Notes

This paper represents a significant step forward compared to classical fault-tolerant control methods.

Unlike previous approaches that either accept spinning flight (Mueller & D'Andrea) or compensate disturbances using passive FTC, this work formulates fault-tolerant control as an optimization problem.

The key idea is that the controller predicts the future behavior of the damaged quadrotor and computes the optimal motor commands while respecting the physical limitations of the remaining actuators.

Compared with previous papers:

- **Paper 1:** Uses Reduced Attitude Control and LQR.
- **Paper 2:** Uses Passive FTC with Disturbance Estimation.
- **Paper 3:** Uses Nonlinear Model Predictive Control with online optimization.

This paper demonstrates how optimization-based control can significantly improve recovery performance after severe rotor failures.

# 9. Research Ideas / My Thoughts

This paper raises several interesting research questions.

### Model Predictive Control

- Can adaptive prediction models improve NMPC performance under modeling uncertainties?
- How can prediction horizons be adjusted online according to flight conditions?
- Can computational complexity be reduced for onboard implementation?

### Reinforcement Learning

- Can Reinforcement Learning learn an MPC-like policy without solving optimization online?
- Can RL approximate the optimal control law generated by NMPC?

### Fault Diagnosis

- Can NMPC be combined with online fault estimation to eliminate the assumption of known rotor failures?
- How would the controller perform under partial rotor degradation instead of complete failure?

### Robust Control

- Can disturbance observers improve robustness against wind disturbances?
- How sensitive is NMPC to model mismatch and parameter uncertainty?

### Future Research

- Can the proposed NMPC framework be extended to modular aerial robots such as MARS or BEATLE?
- Can optimization-based fault-tolerant control be combined with morphing aerial vehicles?
- Can simultaneous actuator and sensor failures be handled within the same optimization framework?

---

# Key Takeaways

- NMPC predicts the future behavior of the damaged quadrotor before computing control inputs.
- Optimization is performed online while respecting actuator constraints.
- The controller directly uses nonlinear quadrotor dynamics.
- Rotor failure recovery is possible even during aggressive maneuvers.
- This paper demonstrates the advantages of optimization-based fault-tolerant control over classical methods.

# Stability and Control of a Quadrocopter Despite the Complete Loss of One, Two, or Three Propellers

**Authors:** Mark W. Mueller, Raffaello D'Andrea

**Conference:** IEEE International Conference on Robotics and Automation (ICRA)

**Year:** 2014

**Research Area:** Fault-Tolerant Control (FTC), Quadrotor Control

---

# 1. Research Problem

Conventional quadrotors rely on four functioning propellers to maintain stable flight. A complete failure of one or more propellers usually results in loss of stability and a crash.

This paper investigates whether a standard quadrotor can continue flying after the complete loss of:

- One propeller
- Two opposite propellers
- Three propellers

while still maintaining position control. The authors aim to develop a fault-tolerant control strategy without requiring additional hardware or redesigning the vehicle. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

---

# 2. Proposed Method

Instead of attempting to maintain full attitude control, the authors intentionally allow the quadrotor to rotate continuously around a fixed body axis.

The main idea is:

- Sacrifice yaw attitude control.
- Maintain control of the reduced attitude (the thrust direction).
- Tilt the spinning axis to generate horizontal motion.
- Adjust the total thrust to maintain altitude.

The controller consists of two cascaded loops:

- **Inner Loop:** Reduced attitude controller
- **Outer Loop:** Position controller

A linearized state-space model is derived around the spinning equilibrium, and an LQR controller is designed for attitude stabilization. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1} :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

---

# 3. Assumptions

The proposed approach assumes:

- Complete rotor failure (not partial degradation)
- High-bandwidth motor dynamics
- Symmetric quadrotor structure
- Linear aerodynamic drag model
- Rotor speeds can be directly controlled
- The failed rotor is already known

# 4. Main Contributions

The paper makes several important contributions:

- Demonstrates stable flight after one rotor failure.
- Demonstrates stable flight after two opposite rotor failures.
- Presents a feasible control strategy for three rotor failures.
- Introduces reduced attitude control instead of full attitude control.
- Derives a linearized state-space model around the spinning equilibrium.
- Performs controllability analysis for each failure scenario.
- Validates the controller experimentally (one and two rotor failures) and through nonlinear simulation (three rotor failures).

# 5. Experimental Results

The experiments demonstrate that:

- The quadrotor successfully recovers after losing one propeller.
- Stable hovering and position tracking are achieved using the remaining propellers.
- Stable flight is also achieved with two opposite propellers.
- The three-propeller failure case is validated in simulation because the available hardware cannot generate enough thrust.

# 6. Limitations

The proposed method still has several limitations:

- Only opposite two-propeller failures are considered.
- Adjacent two-propeller failures are not addressed.
- Rotor fault detection is outside the scope of this paper.
- Only complete failures are considered.
- Continuous spinning makes the method unsuitable for applications requiring stable camera orientation or precise yaw control.
- The single-propeller case requires more thrust than the experimental platform can provide.

# 8. My Notes

This paper is one of the foundational works in quadrotor fault-tolerant control.

Instead of fighting the unavoidable spinning motion after a rotor failure, the authors embrace it and design a controller around this new equilibrium.

The key innovation is the concept of **Reduced Attitude Control**, where only the thrust direction is controlled while the vehicle is allowed to spin freely.

Many later works on:

- Passive Fault-Tolerant Control (PFTC)
- Active Fault-Tolerant Control (AFTC)
- Model Predictive Control (MPC)
- Reinforcement Learning (RL)

are built upon this fundamental idea.

# 9. Research Ideas / My Thoughts

This paper raises several interesting research questions:

### Control

- Can Nonlinear Model Predictive Control (NMPC) improve trajectory tracking during rotor failure?
- Can adaptive control reduce the recovery time after a rotor failure?
- Can the spinning equilibrium be stabilized with better optimization methods?

### Reinforcement Learning

- Can Reinforcement Learning learn the fault-tolerant controller directly?
- Can RL eliminate the need for manually designed LQR controllers?
- Can RL handle multiple fault scenarios using a single policy?

### Fault Diagnosis

- Can rotor failures be detected automatically without dedicated fault sensors?
- Can fault estimation be integrated with the controller for smoother recovery?

### Modular Robotics

- Can this control strategy be extended to modular aerial robots such as MARS or BEATLE?
- How would self-reconfiguration change the control strategy after rotor failure?

### Future Research

- Can a controller tolerate both actuator failures and sensor failures simultaneously?
- Can fault-tolerant control be combined with resilient odometry?
- How can morphing aerial robots improve fault tolerance?

# Key Takeaways

- Losing one rotor does not necessarily mean the quadrotor must crash.
- Full attitude control is sacrificed to preserve stable flight.
- Reduced attitude control is the key concept introduced in this paper.
- A spinning quadrotor can still remain controllable.
- This paper serves as a foundation for many modern fault-tolerant control methods used in aerial robotics.

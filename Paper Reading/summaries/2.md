# Uniform Passive Fault-Tolerant Control for a Quadcopter Subject to One, Two, or Three Rotor Failures

**Authors:** (Original paper authors)

**Journal:** IEEE Transactions on Control Systems Technology

**Research Area:** Fault-Tolerant Control (FTC), UAV Control

---

# 1. Research Problem

Most existing fault-tolerant control (FTC) methods require explicit fault information and controller switching after a rotor failure. These approaches depend on reliable fault diagnosis and may suffer from delayed responses or incorrect fault identification.

This paper investigates whether a quadcopter can maintain stable flight under one, two adjacent, two opposite, or even three rotor failures **without requiring rotor fault information or controller switching**.

---

# 2. Proposed Method

The authors propose a **Uniform Passive Fault-Tolerant Control (PFTC)** framework that uses a single controller for both normal and faulty flight conditions.

Instead of identifying which rotor has failed, rotor failures are modeled as an equivalent **lumped disturbance** acting on the quadcopter's virtual control inputs.

The controller consists of three main components:

- Disturbance estimation using a disturbance observer.
- Passive fault-tolerant controller that compensates for the estimated disturbance.
- Dynamic control allocation that distributes control commands to the remaining motors without switching between different allocation strategies.

Unlike conventional Active FTC methods, the controller does not require fault diagnosis or multiple controller designs.

---

# 3. Assumptions

The proposed method assumes:

- Rotor failures are treated as unknown disturbances.
- No explicit rotor fault information is available.
- Rotor failures may include:
  - One rotor failure
  - Two adjacent rotor failures
  - Two opposite rotor failures
  - Three rotor failures
- The quadcopter dynamics are accurately modeled.
- Disturbances can be estimated in real time.

---

# 4. Main Contributions

The paper makes several important contributions:

- Proposes a **uniform passive fault-tolerant controller** that works from fault-free operation to multiple rotor failures.
- Eliminates the need for rotor fault detection and controller switching.
- Models rotor failures as lumped disturbances and estimates them online.
- Introduces a modified controller structure capable of handling severe rotor failures.
- Employs dynamic control allocation to avoid switching allocation strategies.
- Provides theoretical closed-loop stability analysis.
- Validates the proposed controller through outdoor flight experiments.

---

# 5. Experimental Results

The experimental results demonstrate that:

- The quadcopter successfully recovers after a single rotor failure.
- Stable hovering is achieved after one, two adjacent, two opposite, and three rotor failures.
- The same controller is used throughout all flight conditions.
- No fault information or controller switching is required during recovery.
- Outdoor experiments confirm the practicality of the proposed approach.

---

# 6. Limitations

Several limitations remain:

- The disturbance estimator may exhibit estimation delays during sudden failures.
- The method relies on an accurate system model.
- Rotor fault locations are not explicitly identified.
- Performance may degrade during aggressive maneuvers.
- More advanced estimation techniques could further improve disturbance compensation.

---

# 7. Future Work

Possible future research directions include:

- Integrating learning-based disturbance estimation.
- Combining Passive FTC with Model Predictive Control (MPC).
- Extending the controller to fully nonlinear quadrotor dynamics.
- Improving disturbance estimation under rapidly changing flight conditions.
- Applying the method to modular and morphing aerial robots.

# 8. My Notes

This paper extends the classical fault-tolerant control strategy by removing the dependence on explicit fault diagnosis.

Instead of identifying which rotor has failed, the controller only estimates the overall effect of the failure on the vehicle dynamics. This significantly simplifies the control architecture while maintaining acceptable flight performance.

Compared with the previous paper by Mueller and D'Andrea (2014), which accepts continuous spinning after rotor failure, this work focuses on designing a **single passive controller** capable of handling multiple fault scenarios without controller switching.

This paper serves as an important bridge between classical fault-tolerant control methods and more advanced approaches such as learning-based FTC and nonlinear MPC.

- Dynamic control allocation removes the need for controller switching.
